SECTION 1:
WHEN YOUR MIND AND BODY FEEL LIKE THEY ARE ALWAYS "ON"

Anxiety is more than occasional worry or stress.

It can feel like your internal alarm system never fully turns off, even when nothing is obviously wrong. You may be going through the motions of daily life, showing up for work, caring for others, and handling responsibilities, while internally feeling tense, scattered, or on edge.

For many people, anxiety feels like:

- Racing thoughts or constant overthinking
- A sense of dread or a "something is wrong" feeling
- Difficulty relaxing, even in safe environments
- Muscle tension, restlessness, or feeling physically on edge
- Trouble sleeping or staying asleep
- Difficulty focusing or feeling mentally scattered
- Irritability or emotional sensitivity
- Needing reassurance or control to feel grounded
- Avoiding situations that feel uncertain or overwhelming

Anxiety is not just a mindset issue. It is often a nervous system response that has learned to stay alert in order to stay safe.

That means the goal is not to shame yourself for feeling anxious. The goal is to understand what your system is responding to and help it learn that it does not have to stay in survival mode all the time.

SECTION 2:
ANXIETY IS NOT ALWAYS ABOUT DANGER. SOMETIMES IT IS ABOUT CHANGE.

One of the most overlooked causes of anxiety is life transition, even positive change.

The brain is wired for predictability. It naturally prefers what is familiar, even if what is familiar is stressful, limiting, or uncomfortable. Because of this, meaningful life changes can activate anxiety simply because they bring uncertainty.

Common transitions that can trigger anxiety include:

- Starting or changing careers
- Ending or beginning relationships
- Moving to a new home or city
- Becoming a parent
- Changes in family dynamics or co-parenting arrangements
- Health changes or identity shifts
- Personal growth or entering a new stage of life

Even when these changes are positive, the nervous system may interpret uncertainty as potential threat.

Anxiety, in this way, is often the brain's attempt to create control in the face of unpredictability. Therapy helps you understand this response without judgment, so you can begin to build safety from the inside instead of relying only on external certainty.

SECTION 3:
WHAT ANXIETY THERAPY HELPS WITH

Anxiety therapy focuses on helping you understand your internal alarm system and develop a greater sense of safety within yourself.

Rather than relying only on reassurance, avoidance, or control, therapy helps you build the capacity to stay connected to yourself even when life feels uncertain.

In therapy, we work toward:

- Reducing chronic worry and mental overactivity
- Calming physical symptoms of anxiety and hyperarousal
- Increasing emotional regulation and distress tolerance
- Building comfort with uncertainty and change
- Understanding triggers and patterns of activation
- Strengthening confidence and internal grounding
- Improving sleep, focus, and daily functioning
- Creating more flexibility in how your nervous system responds

The goal is not to eliminate anxiety completely.

The goal is to help anxiety become less controlling, less disruptive, and less dominant in your life.

SECTION 4:
MY APPROACH TO ANXIETY THERAPY

I use a trauma-informed, attachment-based, and nervous system focused approach to anxiety.

This means we do not only work on changing thoughts. We also work with the deeper emotional and physiological patterns that keep anxiety active.

You are not treated like a list of symptoms. Your anxiety is understood in context: your history, relationships, nervous system, coping strategies, past experiences, and current life stressors.

This approach may include Internal Family Systems, EMDR, and AEDP.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 5:
INTERNAL FAMILY SYSTEMS (IFS) FOR ANXIETY

From an IFS perspective, anxiety is often connected to protective parts of you that are trying to anticipate problems, prevent mistakes, avoid rejection, or maintain control.

These parts are not the enemy.

They are often trying to keep you safe based on past experiences.

In therapy, we get curious about these anxious parts rather than trying to suppress them. We listen for what they fear, what they are protecting, and what they need in order to soften.

As these parts feel understood and supported, they often become less extreme and more flexible.

This creates more internal space, calm, and self-trust.

SECTION 6:
EMDR THERAPY FOR ANXIETY

Anxiety is sometimes fueled by past experiences where the nervous system learned that uncertainty, vulnerability, conflict, rejection, or unpredictability was unsafe.

Even when life is different now, the body may still respond as if danger is present.

EMDR helps process these experiences so they are no longer stored in the body as present-day threat responses.

This can help your system respond more appropriately to current situations, rather than reacting as if the past is happening again.

EMDR can be especially helpful when anxiety feels intense, body-based, automatic, or connected to earlier experiences you cannot simply think your way out of.

SECTION 7:
AEDP FOR ANXIETY

AEDP focuses on helping you safely experience and process emotions that may have previously felt overwhelming or unmanageable.

Anxiety often builds when emotions are avoided, rushed past, minimized, or left unprocessed.

Through a supportive therapeutic relationship, AEDP helps create emotional safety so your system can move from tension and fear toward clarity, relief, and grounded emotional experience.

This is not about forcing vulnerability.

It is about creating enough safety for your system to process what it has been holding.

--------------------------------------------------------------------------------

SECTION 8:
WHAT TO EXPECT IN ANXIETY THERAPY

Anxiety therapy is not about pushing yourself to "stop worrying."

It is about helping your nervous system learn that it does not need to stay in a constant state of alertness to keep you safe.

In our work together, you can expect:

- A calm, steady, and supportive therapeutic environment
- Tools to regulate physical and emotional anxiety symptoms
- Exploration of thought patterns without judgment
- Work with underlying emotional and relational triggers
- Gentle exposure to uncertainty in a safe and supported way
- Increased self-awareness and internal grounding over time

The goal is to help your system shift from survival mode into a more flexible and grounded state of being.

SECTION 9:
YOU DO NOT NEED TO WAIT UNTIL ANXIETY FEELS "UNMANAGEABLE"

Anxiety often becomes more intense when it is ignored, minimized, or pushed through alone.

Therapy can be a space to understand what your nervous system is responding to and why, even if you do not have all the answers yet.

If you often feel tense, overwhelmed, emotionally sensitive, or unable to fully relax, even when life looks "fine" on the outside, that experience is worth paying attention to.

You do not have to manage it alone.

FAQ 1:
What is anxiety therapy?

Answer:
Anxiety therapy helps you understand the patterns, triggers, and nervous system responses that keep anxiety active. It may include emotional regulation tools, deeper processing work, and support for building more internal safety and confidence.

FAQ 2:
Can EMDR help with anxiety?

Answer:
EMDR may help when anxiety is connected to past experiences, unresolved stress, trauma, or body-based threat responses. It supports the nervous system in processing experiences that may still feel active in the present.

FAQ 3:
Is anxiety always caused by trauma?

Answer:
No. Anxiety can be connected to trauma, stress, attachment patterns, life transitions, uncertainty, biology, or a combination of factors. Therapy helps you understand what is driving your anxiety specifically.

FAQ 4:
Do I need to have panic attacks to start anxiety therapy?

Answer:
No. You do not need to wait until anxiety becomes severe. Therapy can help with overthinking, tension, worry, avoidance, emotional sensitivity, and difficulty relaxing.
